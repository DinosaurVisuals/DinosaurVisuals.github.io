ARchipalago Group Project


Overview project

ARchipalago is an AR project along user to explore a living, interactive island where your choices shape the ecosystem! User can use AR technology to see how different factors impact the land, water, and wildlife.

The Goals of the project:

  • Understand how ecosystems respond to human actions and natural changes
  • Explore and learn about the role of mangroves, coral nurseries, wind farms, and fishing activity
  • Experiment with different variables and see their effects in real time

Jan 29, 2025

BrainStorming

In our first meeting, we brainstorming various interactive educational models designed to enhance learning in biomedical science. The discussion focused on utilizing 3D-printed interactive models and digital applications to create engaging, hands-on experiences. The proposed topics cover ecosystem dynamics, molecular interactions, reproduction, and immune responses, each offering a unique way to visualize complex biological and environmental processes. Below is a summary of the key ideas discussed.

  • Ecosystem: Add different factors caused from human activity to observe the ecosystem changes.
  • Molecular Reactions: Demonstrate different chemical interactions and their outcomes.
  • Reproduction: Show sperm-egg interaction, explaining the biological basis of twins, triplets, and other multiple births.
  • Immune Response: Demonstrate antibody-virus binding and macrophage responses to foreign cells.

Following discussions, we have decided to focus on Ecosystem topic due to its greater potential for interactivity and the opportunity to explore topics we haven't covered in previous assignments.

Feb 06, 2025

Research and Discussion

The discussion focused on key elements of the island ecosystem project. The project plans to include different island types with 3D-printed puzzle pieces featuring scannable images for an AR experience. Coral bleaching and mangrove ecosystems were highlighted, emphasizing their role in biodiversity, coastal protection, and economic and cultural value. A central island piece will allow users to "plant" mangroves to explore their positive impact. Negative elements include a fishing boat representing fish population depletion and ghost gear, as well as the effects of wind farms and industrial agriculture, such as habitat loss, soil degradation, and algae blooms that disrupt marine life. The project will feature four effector pieces: mangrove planting (positive), wind farms (negative impact on birds and bats), fishing boats (negative impact on fish populations and plastic pollution), and coral reef nurseries (illustrating shading effects). The island piece will have divots for attaching interactive elements, with potential AR integration if time allows.

Summary of the four effectors:

  • Mangrove planting: Enhances coastal ecosystems by providing nursery grounds for marine life, reducing erosion, and protecting against natural disasters.
  • Coral reef nursery: Supports coral restoration efforts, though shading in nurseries can impact coral growth.
  • Wind farm: Impacts bird and bat populations, potentially disrupting local ecosystems.
  • Industrial fishing: Leads to fish population depletion, disrupts the food chain, and introduces ghost gear (abandoned fishing equipment).

Feb 28, 2025

3D Assets Creation

Today, Naomi and I worked together at UTM with the goal of completing all the 3D assets. We wanted to have everything ready for Jeah, our amazing developer, so they would have more time to explore Unity. I was responsible for creating and animating the boat, coral reef, palm tree, land, and ocean, as well as integrating all the assets into the final scene. Naomi took on the remaining assets, including the bird, bat, fish, and mangroves. All models follow our design style guide, meaning they are low-poly and need to be structured in a way that allows for proper exploration and display within the project.


i) Problem and Solution:

Problem 01:
The first problem I face is how to effectively indicate that the boat is a fishing boat? I needed a way to clearly communicate that the boat was a fishing vessel without spending too much time on modeling. Using a string was problematic—it could not be too thick, or it would not look like a string, but if it was too thin, it would be hard to see on the final screen. Creating a net was another option, but it would have been too time-consuming.
Solution 01:
Instead of using a string or net, I decided to model a metal chain for the boat. This effectively represents its function while addressing the visibility and modeling concerns.

Problem 02:
I initially tried to create an anchor and metal chain with a hook by extruding shapes from an AI file. However, the extrusion process resulted in weird faces, and when I exported the boat as an OBJ file, the spaces between each chain link and hook closed incorrectly. The anchor also did not function as expected.
Solution 02:
To fix this, I recreated the chain and hook directly in Maya, which resolved the issue. I also decided to exclude the anchor since it was not a key asset. My main takeaway from this challenge is that if a model is not too complex, it's often best to create it entirely in Maya. If I do start from an AI file, I need to minimize the number of points in the shape to avoid extrusion issues.


ii) Adjustment and Refinements

I reused a coral reef asset I had previously created for another project. However, the original model included detailed textures and was designed with smooth shading applied in Maya, as it didn't need to be exported. For this project, the coral reef had to be exported as a low-poly model without smooth shading. I revisited the original Maya file and modified the model to meet these requirements. I applied the same polygon reduction process to the palm tree model as well.


iii) New things I learned:

Create polygon in Maya

I usually start modeling from a cube, but for the low-poly rocks, I learned a new technique from an online tutorial. Instead of starting with a cube, I drew a polygon plane and extruded it in Maya. This method is useful for creating irregular shapes, and I'll consider using it in future projects when working with organic or non-standard forms.

Link: Low-poly rock tutorial
Create wave in Maya

While working on the wave asset, I also explored different ways to animate the waves in Maya. I tested two approaches for generating wave textures and animations:
- Texture deformer: For a regular, repeating wave pattern, Used Deform → Texture → Ocean to create consistent wave motion (F2)
- Wave deformer: For a more controllable wave animation, Used Deform → Nonlinear → Wave Deformer to customize the wave behavior (F3)

Link: Low-polyWave Tutorial
Link: TextureDeformer Tutorial

In our project, we plan to implement two wave states:
- Large, powerful waves when no mangroves are present on the island.
- Smaller, more regular waves when mangroves are planted, demonstrating their role in wave reduction.

Test on 3D Printing Model

On Thursday, David introduced us to the 3D printing machine at BMC. The next day, I tested the process using the boat model I created for this project, and it turned out really well! Huge-thanks to David for his help!

Key considerations Before Printing:

- Ensure that no normal faces are reversed.
- Make sure each part of the model is mostly attached to the main structure to prevent it from detaching during printing.
- Position delicate details, such as the logo on the boat, facing upward in the pre-printing software to avoid support materials attaching to the surface.
- Slightly tilt the object to minimize the amount of support material required.

Initially, Naomi planned to create the 3D island model and fact pieces, while I was responsible for the printing process. However, she wanted to try the 3D printing process herself, so she took on the actual 3D-printing model for the project instead.

Mar 08, 2025

Animate 3D assets & Export FBX files for Developer

The animation process is always the most exciting part for me, but it can also be quite challenging at times. For this project, I animated the boat, fish, and bird using motion paths, constraining them to loop around the island. To animate the planting of young mangroves, I used MASH, allowing each mangrove to appear individually and be planted into the ground. For the wave animation, we decided to create it directly in Unity, as it would be easier for the developer to control both the wave movement and texture changes. This was my first time exporting animated objects using motion paths and deformers, and I initially didnt realize that I needed to bake all the animations before exporting. After looking into it, I followed this tutorial on baking animations for FBX files:

Link: Bake animation for FBX files

Once I baked the animations and exported them as FBX files, I tested them on Sketchfab before handing them off to the developer. The first issue I encountered was that the textures were not visible on Sketchfab, but fortunately, Jeah confirmed that they appeared correctly in Unity. The second issue was that the mangrove animation did not function as expected. After researching the issue, I learned that MASH animations need to be converted to an instancer before baking. With these adjustments, the animations are now correctly exported and ready for use in Unity (maybe....).

Wave Animation Issue & Solutions

After this week's group meeting, we decided to animate the wave in Maya instead of Unity. The texture of the wave in Unity didn’t match the style of the other assets, and creating a controllable wave in Unity seemed challenging. Based on my exploration from February 28, I created the wave using Wave Deformer and Texture Deformer, then keyframed the frequency. However, I made the mistake of not testing the wave animation on Sketchfab before sending it to Jeah. A few days later, we realized that Wave Deformer and Texture Deformer cannot be baked into an FBX file, causing the animation to break.

Potential Solutions:

01 Animate the wave in Unity (back to Jeah's approach)
• Pros: Would avoid the baking issue.
• Cons: The wave texture might not match the other assets, and the surface texture could block the coral and fish underneath. Also, animating the wave impact would add extra workload for Jeah.
02 Manually bend and keyframe each stage of the wave in Maya
• Pros: Ensures consistency in style and visibility of underwater assets.
• Cons: Requires patience and time to keyframe each stage smoothly.

Decision: Test Solution 2 and see if it produces a smooth and natural wave motion.

Mar 19, 2025

Wave animation try and error

Baking Deformation : https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=c42mRMYWNbE

I followed these steps to create and export the wave animation as an FBX file:

- Use deformers to shape and control the wave frequency. - Copy each stage of the wave animation separately. - Blend the stages into a single object. - Keyframe each bending stage. - Bake the bend animation. - Export as an FBX file.

To ensure Jeah has enough time to embed the big wave, small wave, and contaminated wave animations in Unity, I aimed to complete them today. To save time, I kept the animation loop short, ensuring the wave shape at the start and end matched a neutral position. This way, the loop would feel more seamless. Finally, I finished the different wave types. They weren’t perfectly smooth, but they were functional and ready to be passed on to Jeah. After a quick test in Unity, the waves seemed to be working fine!

Developer Feedback:


• Instead of creating four separate wave animations, I could try making one long animation with all variations in a single file, making it easier to control in Unity.
• Next time, I should rename object layers and texture files in Maya to prevent naming conflicts, which can cause issues when reading the files in Unity.
